On April 14, 2026, the Energy and Petroleum Regulatory Authority published its monthly fuel price review. Super petrol in Nairobi rose by KES 28.69 per litre to KES 206.97. Diesel climbed KES 40.30 to KES 206.84, a 24% increase in a single review cycle. Kerosene held unchanged at KES 152.78.

The government moved quickly to soften the optics. VAT on petroleum products was trimmed from 16% to 13%, and KES 6.2 billion from the Petroleum Development Levy Fund was deployed as a subsidy. Energy CS Opiyo Wandayi said that without both interventions, prices would have gone higher still. The next day, President Ruto promised to cut VAT further to 8% within three months.

Both announcements have a legal problem.

## What the Law Actually Says

Kenya's VAT Act, 2013, Section 5(2)(b) sets the standard VAT rate at 16%. Section 6(1) caps any executive variation at 25% of that rate. The arithmetic is straightforward: 25% of 16% is 4 percentage points. The maximum the government can move VAT without parliamentary approval is therefore down to 12%.

Ruto's promise to cut to 8% requires a reduction of 8 percentage points, which is double the cap the law allows the executive to cross unilaterally. Delivering on that promise requires Parliament to amend the VAT Act itself, not a Legal Notice from the National Treasury. Legal Notice No. 70, published April 15 to implement the initial cut to 8% that EPRA briefly applied before revising to 13%, was on legally questionable ground the moment it was signed.

The opposition is not wrong when it calls for a special parliamentary sitting. That sitting is not a political demand. Under the current legal framework, it is a procedural requirement for any VAT adjustment beyond the 12% floor.

The landed cost argument is real. Between February and March 2026, the landed cost of super petrol rose 41.53%, diesel 68.72%, and kerosene 105.15%, driven directly by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz following US and Israeli strikes on Iran in late February. Kenya imports all of its refined fuel. There is no domestic production buffer. When global prices move that sharply, pump prices follow.

None of that changes what the VAT Act says about who can authorise a rate change of that magnitude. External shocks do not suspend constitutional process.

## The Manufactured Shortage

Before the price review published, the shortages had already started.

As early as April 7, motorists across Nairobi were circling the city looking for fuel. Kericho town was down to a single operational petrol station. Nyamira reported multiple closures. In Trans Nzoia, stations in Kitale, Kiminini, Kachibora, and Endebess were overwhelmed or shut. At one Homa Bay station, an attendant was filling plastic water bottles because the regular dispensers had run dry.

Kenya Pipeline Company figures released April 8 told a different story from what motorists were experiencing on the ground. The Kipevu Oil Storage terminal in Mombasa held 29,648 cubic metres of super petrol. Kenya Petroleum Refineries held 64,742 cubic metres. There was no supply crisis in the national stockpile.

The Motorist Association of Kenya said what most people had already concluded: the shortage was artificial. Petrol stations were sitting on inventory and waiting for the April 14 price review to publish before selling it, knowing that every litre sold at the old price was a litre sold below the incoming price. The incentive to hoard was not subtle.

EPRA confirmed the shortage in Nairobi and Machakos on April 9 and reminded petroleum dealers that hoarding is a criminal offence. The Competition Authority of Kenya issued a warning to oil marketing companies on April 10, stating that deliberate withholding of supply to create artificial scarcity violates Kenya's competition laws.

Both statements had the tone of institutions that knew exactly what was happening and were not in a position to stop it. No station was closed. No company was prosecuted. The April 14 review arrived on schedule and prices moved as the market had predicted.

In Eldoret, farmers watched tractors sit idle as the rains came. Planting windows that cannot be recovered were lost to a shortage that the available stock data shows should not have existed.

## The Political Arithmetic

The United Alternative Government opposition coalition, aligned with Rigathi Gachagua, Kalonzo Musyoka, Fred Matiangi, and Justin Muturi, called on April 15 for a special parliamentary sitting within seven days, the cancellation of the Government-to-Government petroleum import framework, and the immediate removal of VAT on fuel products entirely.

MP Ndindi Nyoro put forward a more detailed counter-proposal: a KES 5 billion subsidy top-up, a five-percentage-point VAT reduction, and the removal of the KES 7-per-litre fuel levy introduced in 2024, which he estimated would collectively reduce pump prices by up to KES 27 per litre. Kenya consumes roughly 400 million litres of fuel monthly, which gives a sense of the fiscal cost of sustained intervention at that scale.

The Matatu Owners Association announced a 25% fare increase the same day, citing diesel costs. A vehicle earning around KES 8,000 daily now burns through an extra KES 2,400 in fuel. That arithmetic has already passed from operator balance sheets to passenger pockets.

## What This Actually Is

Kenya's fuel crisis in April 2026 has three distinct layers.

The first is external and genuine: Hormuz disruptions caused a real supply shock and a real cost increase. No domestic policy choice caused the landed cost of diesel to rise 69% in a month.

The second is structural and self-inflicted: a pricing and tax framework that creates near-perfect conditions for hoarding by giving market participants advance certainty about the direction of price changes, then provides no enforcement mechanism capable of acting before the review date passes.

The third is legal and unresolved: a government that bypassed parliamentary procedure to adjust VAT rates beyond the statutory cap, then announced further adjustments that the law as written does not permit the executive to make alone.

The external shock is beyond anyone's control. The other two are policy choices. They deserve to be treated as such.
