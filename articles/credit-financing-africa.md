It starts simply enough.

A bodaboda rider in Nairobi needs a smartphone to run deliveries through an app. He cannot afford KES 18,000 upfront. A shop sells it to him on a daily payment plan — KES 300 a day for 90 days. That is KES 27,000 total. The implied annual interest rate is somewhere north of 150 percent. He signs nothing. He understands the daily number, not the total cost. The transaction is complete in under five minutes.

This is the entry point of the African credit ladder. It gets steeper from here.

## The Ladder

The progression from accessible to predatory credit in Sub-Saharan Africa follows a pattern that is consistent enough to be structural.

**Mobile micro-loans** sit at the bottom rung. M-Shwari, Tala, Branch, Fuliza — apps that disburse in minutes using M-Pesa transaction history as a credit proxy. The loans are small, typically under KES 50,000, and the repayment windows are short, usually 30 days. The stated fees look innocuous. M-Shwari charges a flat 7.5 percent facilitation fee per loan. Annualised, that is 395 percent. Tala and Branch annualise at 132 to 152 percent respectively. Kenya's Central Bank governor Patrick Njoroge publicly compared these platforms to shylocks. The comparison was accurate enough that no one seriously disputed it.

The mechanics of the trap are straightforward. A borrower who cannot repay within 30 days rolls over the loan and pays the fee again on the outstanding balance. Interest compounds on a principal that has not shrunk. The debt grows faster than any micro-business can service it. A 2019 survey found 2.7 million Kenyans had been negatively listed with credit reference bureaux, 400,000 of them for amounts under two dollars.

**Microfinance institutions and SACCOs** occupy the next rung. Interest rates run between 18 and 36 percent annually, which sounds moderate after mobile lending rates but is still punishing for a business with thin margins. The collateral requirements are lighter than commercial banks but they exist — a guarantor, group liability, or in the case of larger MFI loans, a physical asset. Repayment schedules are weekly or monthly and inflexible. Missing a payment triggers penalties. Missing several triggers recovery proceedings.

**Commercial banks** should come next on the ladder. For most MSMEs, they do not. Banks in Kenya, Nigeria, Ghana, and across the region have systematically retreated from MSME lending, preferring government securities, real estate financing, and exposure to listed equities in Europe and North America — assets with predictable returns and no relationship management overhead. The IFC-World Bank MSME Finance Gap Report published in March 2025 put the financing shortfall for small enterprises across emerging markets at USD 5.65 trillion. Sub-Saharan Africa's share of that gap is estimated at USD 100 billion annually. Banks are not filling it. They have made a rational institutional decision that they will not.

**Informal lenders and asset-backed loans** fill the vacuum. These are the operators who were always called shylocks before the term migrated to describing mobile apps. Rates of 30 percent per month are common. Collateral is title deeds, vehicle logbooks, and in some cases livestock. The enforcement mechanisms are extralegal and swift. Default does not result in a court filing. It results in the logbook being surrendered and the car disappearing before the borrower processes what happened.

The structure creates a compounding problem even for borrowers who do not default. A trader servicing a 30 percent monthly loan is paying 360 percent per year on a principal that is supposed to be generating enough return to cover both the interest and a living. The mathematics of this do not work for any business with a margin below 30 percent monthly — which is most businesses, always.

## Why Banks Look Away

The absence of commercial bank credit from this picture is not an accident of history. It is a consequence of incentive structures that have never pointed toward small enterprise lending.

A bank officer processing an MSME loan must assess a business with informal accounts, irregular cash flows, and limited transaction history. The due diligence cost for a KES 500,000 loan is roughly the same as for a KES 50 million loan. The fee income is not. The risk-adjusted return on a government bond or a mortgage to a salaried professional is measurably superior. Banks are not charities. They optimise for return on equity, and MSME lending has never compared favorably.

This is compounded by the structure of bank balance sheets across the region. Kenyan tier-1 banks — which posted record profits in FY2025 — held significant portions of their asset bases in government securities and regional sovereign debt. Nigerian banks have historically maintained large offshore currency positions. Ghanaian banks loaded up on domestic government paper during the fiscal crises of the early 2020s. Capital that could theoretically finance productive small enterprise sits instead in instruments whose risk profile regulators and shareholders find more comfortable.

## What Actually Exists

The alternatives to this system are real but underdeveloped.

**SACCOs** remain the most genuinely accessible formal credit for small enterprises in East Africa. They are member-owned, operate at lower margins than commercial banks, and lend against savings history rather than collateral. The limitation is scale: most SACCOs operate within narrow geographic or professional boundaries and cannot serve businesses outside their membership base.

**Revenue-based financing** is emerging as a structurally better product for MSMEs than debt. Instead of fixed monthly repayments, the lender takes a fixed percentage of monthly revenue until the advance is repaid. Repayments flex with the business cycle. A bad month means a smaller payment, not a default. Platforms like JUMO, which has disbursed over USD 4 billion in loans across six African markets, are moving in this direction. The product is not yet widely available at small-enterprise scale.

**Credit guarantee schemes** reduce bank risk without reducing bank returns, theoretically making MSME lending more attractive. The African Guarantee Fund has provided coverage for loans to thousands of enterprises that commercial banks would not otherwise have touched. The constraint is that guarantee coverage is finite and the institutions administering it cannot scale fast enough to close a USD 100 billion gap through guarantee mechanisms alone.

**Blended finance structures** combining concessional development capital with commercial debt can bring effective borrowing costs down significantly. These work well for mid-market enterprises with governance structures capable of satisfying development finance institution reporting requirements. They do not work for the bodaboda rider or the market trader, whose financing needs are too small and too informal to interface with institutional capital.

## The Honest Assessment

None of the alternatives currently available operates at the scale needed to displace predatory lending as the primary source of credit for African MSMEs. The gap between what the productive small enterprise sector needs and what the formal financial system will provide is measured in the hundreds of billions of dollars annually.

Closing it requires three things happening simultaneously: regulation that puts a floor under lending rates without eliminating access altogether, product innovation that prices credit to business cash flows rather than calendar months, and institutional capital that accepts the risk profile of small enterprise lending rather than routing around it into safer alternatives.

The technology exists. The capital exists. The regulatory frameworks are being built, slowly. What is missing is the institutional will to treat small enterprise finance as a development priority rather than a residual category that the market will eventually figure out on its own.

It has had several decades to figure it out. The gap is wider now than when the conversation started.
