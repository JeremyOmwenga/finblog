Started in 2026 as a personal log of a career moving from financial analysis into quantitative methods and tax policy, with a persistent side interest in what African institutions could learn from the rest of the world.

Raison d'être

This site was built to be a record of ideas encountered, positions formed and revised, and the slow accumulation of understanding that a career in modern finance requires. Writing forces clarity. Publishing raises the stakes just enough to be useful.

The working title reflects the original impulse of tracking markets, understanding price formation, and documenting what the numbers say about the economies that produce them. That scope has widened to cater for Tax policy, Quantitative methods, and the institutional architecture of financial systems in Sub-Saharan Africa have become equally central concerns.

These are not separate tracks moreso they converge on a question that feels increasingly urgent: why African financial systems consistently underprice and underserve the productive economy. What would it take to change that?

The institutional gap

Sub-Saharan Africa has the fastest-growing SME sector in the world by headcount. It also has some of the most expensive and least accessible credit on the planet. The gap between those two facts is not a straightforward market failure, more of a design failure. The institutions, instruments, and incentive structures that would connect patient capital to productive small enterprises simply do not exist at meaningful scale.

They exist elsewhere. The western financial system, for all its well-documented excesses, has spent decades building infrastructure for exactly this problem. Community development financial institutions in the United States. Regional guarantee schemes underpinning Germany's Mittelstand ecosystem. Revenue-based financing structures that align lender and borrower incentives without demanding collateral that most small businesses do not own. The SBA loan guarantee model alone, as imperfect as it is, has channelled hundreds of billions into enterprises that conventional banks would not touch. The UK's British Business Bank operates on similar logic. So does the European Investment Fund's SME guarantee window.

None of this is a case for wholesale importation of western financial architecture. Context matters deeply. Informal economies, thin credit bureau coverage, and the structural dominance of agriculture and trade in SSA's SME sector all require adaptation, not copy-paste. But adaptation requires first understanding what you are adapting from and that understanding remains patchy among the institutions that need it most.

The question is not whether African institutions can afford to adopt better credit models. It is whether they can afford not to, given what the current model costs the productive economy every year it persists.

A specific concern

Predatory credit financing is the most corrosive feature of the current landscape. Interest rates of 25–40% on short-term SME loans which are common across Kenya, Nigeria, Ghana, and Tanzania do not finance growth. They finance survival at best, and debt traps at worst. The small trader borrowing at 35% annual interest to stock inventory is not building a business. She is subsidising a bank's return on equity with her own labour and risk.

The mechanisms that constrain predatory lending in more developed markets such as regulatory rate structures, alternative lender competition, credit union infrastructure, government-backed guarantee schemes are either absent or too weak to matter across most of Sub-Saharan Africa. Building them is slow, unglamorous policy work. It is also among the highest-return investments any of these governments could make in their own productive capacity.

This site will document, argue about, and occasionally celebrate progress on that front, alongside markets, data, and everything else that seems worth writing down.
