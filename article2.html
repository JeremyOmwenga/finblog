Historic KPC IPO or Overvalued?

Jeremy Angwenyi 3 min read 700 words

KPC is a giant, debuting at 6th with Market Cap. 166.8 billion. However, the National Infrastructure Fund may greatly compromise medium-term objectives.

The Kenya Pipeline Company IPO was a test. And the market failed it. Not because investors don’t have money, but because the government asked for too much. Here are the facts. The IPO was priced at nine shillings per share. Independent analysts, including firms from Uganda who actually rely on this pipeline, valued it between four and five shillings. That is nearly a 50 percent difference. The government’s own transaction advisors were the only ones who agreed with the nine shilling price. Investors noticed. Twenty-four hours before the deadline, only ten percent of the shares had been bought. Not ninety percent. Ten percent. That is not a slow start. That is a verdict. The government’s response was to say Kenyans are last-minute people. That they will rush in at the end. But investment decisions are not the same as voter registration. When you are putting your money on the line, you do the math first. And the math here was simple. At nine shillings, Kenya Pipeline was being priced at 22 times its earnings. For context, Safaricom trades at eight to nine times earnings. Kenya Power trades at just over one times earnings. So the government was asking the public to pay more than double for a state-owned pipeline what they would pay for the country’s most profitable telecommunications company. The numbers did not add up. There were other red flags. A corruption investigation is ongoing at the company. There is a three billion shilling environmental lawsuit over pipeline leaks. The company underspent its capital budget by nearly half. Unions went on strike last year. These are not abstract risks. They are real operational problems that affect the value of the business. But the biggest issue was trust. The money from this IPO was going straight to the Treasury for infrastructure projects. It was not going to strengthen the pipeline company itself. So investors were being asked to buy shares in a company that would not benefit from the proceeds of its own sale. The former Central Bank chairman said the process lacked transparency. A senator went to court to stop it over constitutional concerns. And every independent analyst who looked at the numbers arrived at the same conclusion: this thing is overpriced. Now look at the bigger picture. Kenya has not had a successful IPO in eleven years. The last one managed only 28 percent of its target. Meanwhile, Kenyan startups raised nearly a billion dollars last year. That money is flowing into venture capital, private equity, and debt deals. It is funding energy companies and financial technology firms. But that capital is not touching the Nairobi Stock Exchange. Why would it? The public market offers overpriced state assets with valuation gaps and governance concerns. The private market offers growth. The government marketed this IPO like a political campaign. The President personally urged people to buy. The Treasury Secretary held roadshows. They went to Uganda to pitch to stakeholders. They made it available on mobile phones so anyone could buy with as little as two hundred shillings. But you cannot market your way out of a bad valuation. Presidential endorsements do not change the price-to-earnings ratio. Roadshows do not fix a corruption investigation. And mobile apps do not make investors ignore a 50 percent downside warning from a regional partner. The ten percent subscription rate is the market speaking clearly. It is saying we are not buying this price. It is saying do not treat us like last-minute voters. It is saying if you want to sell public assets, price them properly. Some observers suggested canceling the IPO and retendering it at around four or five shillings. That is the right call. Because investor confidence cannot be forced. It has to be earned. And right now, the gap between what the government thinks its assets are worth and what the market is willing to pay is simply too wide. Kenya has the capital. The startup numbers prove that. What it does not have is the trust. And until the pricing reflects reality, that trust will not return.
