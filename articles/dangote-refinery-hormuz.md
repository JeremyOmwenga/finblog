On February 11, 2026, the Dangote Petroleum Refinery in Lekki hit 650,000 barrels per day — full nameplate capacity, the largest single-train refinery ever built. Seventeen days later, US and Israeli airstrikes on Iran effectively closed the Strait of Hormuz. A fifth of the world's daily oil supply stopped moving.

The timing is striking. Whether it amounts to anything depends on questions the refinery cannot answer alone.

## What Hormuz Means for Africa

Roughly 75% of refined fuel imported by East and Southern African countries originates in the Middle East. Ethiopia, Tanzania, Kenya — most of their fuel comes from UAE, Saudi, and Kuwaiti refineries. With the strait closed, that supply chain has fractured. Brent spiked past $120 before settling around $92. The poorest economies on the continent, net importers with thin foreign exchange buffers, are absorbing the shock.

This is exactly the vulnerability Dangote's refinery is positioned to partially address.

## What It Can Actually Offer

About 25% of current output is available for export — roughly 456,000 tonnes shipped in the first wave, delivered to Côte d'Ivoire, Cameroon, Ghana, Togo, and Tanzania. The remaining 75% is reserved for Nigeria's domestic market, where the refinery has already meaningfully cut the country's fuel import bill.

African governments have noticed. South Africa is reportedly in discussions for a 12-month supply contract. Inquiries are up sharply across the continent. The refinery is fielding demand it could not have absorbed six months ago.

For West and Central Africa, the substitution case is straightforward — the geography works, the logistics are proven, and the first cargoes are already moving. East Africa is harder. Routing fuel from Lagos to Mombasa adds cost, though the Hormuz premium on Middle Eastern supply has temporarily closed that gap.

## The Honest Complications

The refinery is partially fed by American crude imports, because Nigerian domestic production cannot consistently supply it at scale. That is a real irony — and a real vulnerability when global shipping is strained by the same conflict creating the export opportunity.

Domestically, Dangote raised Nigerian petrol prices 61% in three weeks as global crude costs fed through. Full capacity does not insulate you from the market you are buying crude on.

And distribution remains the hard problem. A refinery in Lagos cannot substitute overnight for decades of Middle Eastern supply relationships, storage infrastructure, and downstream logistics across a continent.

## The Point

Dangote built this refinery to make money and end Nigeria's dependence on importing refined products from its own crude. He did not build it to be Africa's strategic energy reserve. But timing confers significance that intent does not.

Africa now has a large, domestically-owned refinery at full capacity at the exact moment its traditional supply chain has broken. That is a different and better position than the continent has ever been in. Whether the distribution networks, financing arrangements, and political will to build intra-African energy trade can keep pace is the real question — and that answer is still being written.
