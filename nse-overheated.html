Is the NSE Overheated?

Jeremy Angwenyi 2 min read 384 words

A look at whether recent Nairobi bourse gains reflect fundamentals or euphoria.

Recent gains on the Nairobi Securities Exchange have prompted a familiar debate among local investors: are we watching genuine value creation, or a market running ahead of itself?

The Numbers

The NSE 20 Share Index has climbed steadily through early 2026, buoyed by renewed foreign investor interest and a stabilising shilling. On the surface, this looks like a recovery story. But a closer look at price-to-earnings ratios across the top counters tells a more complicated tale.

Several blue-chip stocks are now trading at multiples that would have seemed ambitious even two years ago. Safaricom, historically the market's anchor, is priced for growth that its recent subscriber data does not obviously support.

What the Bulls Say

Optimists point to the macroeconomic backdrop. Inflation is cooling. The Central Bank of Kenya has signalled a more accommodative stance. And the government's infrastructure push — however contentious its financing — is expected to ripple through to corporate earnings over the next eighteen months.

There is also a regional story here. With Nairobi positioning itself as the financial hub of East Africa, capital flows from outside the continent are increasingly finding their way onto the NSE. That structural demand is real, and it does not disappear with a bad quarterly result.

What the Bears Say

Sceptics are less convinced. They note that much of the recent buying has been concentrated in a handful of counters, leaving the broader market thin and illiquid. Retail participation remains low. And the macroeconomic tailwinds, while real, are priced in — perhaps twice over.

The KPC IPO is the sharpest expression of this tension. A debut at a market cap north of 160 billion shillings is a bold statement for a parastatal operating in a regulated, capital-intensive sector. The National Infrastructure Fund's structural role in that deal raises questions about price discovery that have not been fully answered.

A Middle View

Markets can be both fairly valued and fragile at the same time. The NSE is not obviously in bubble territory, but it has less margin for error than it did twelve months ago. Investors adding exposure here should be doing so with a clear thesis on specific counters — not on the assumption that the tide will keep rising.

The fundamentals, in other words, still matter. They always did.
