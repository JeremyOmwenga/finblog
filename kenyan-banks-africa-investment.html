Record Profits, Redirected Capital: What Kenya's Banks and Africa's Investment Surge Actually Mean

Jeremy Angwenyi 6 min read 1,033 words

Kenya's tier 1 banks just posted their strongest results in history. At the same time, private equity and development capital are flowing into African infrastructure at a scale not seen before. The two stories are connected.

The numbers coming out of Kenya's banking sector for FY2025 are hard to ignore. Equity Group Holdings posted a 55% surge in profit after tax to KES 75.5 billion — one of the strongest single-year performances in the history of Kenyan banking. KCB Group followed with a record KES 68.4 billion PAT, up 10.6%, the highest in the bank's history. Co-operative Bank grew profit before tax by 15.8% to KES 40.3 billion. Combined, the seven largest listed banks returned KES 111.3 billion to shareholders — up 30% from the prior year.

These are genuinely impressive figures. They are also worth interrogating.

How the Profits Were Made

The headline driver across the sector is interest income in a high-rate environment. Kenya's central bank held rates elevated through most of 2025 as inflation remained sticky, and the banks benefited — wider lending margins, strong returns on government securities, and fee income from a payments infrastructure now handling transactions at remarkable scale. KCB reported that 99% of its customer transactions in 2025 happened digitally. For every 100 transactions, one happened at a branch. The cost savings from that shift fed directly into the profit line.

Regional expansion is the other engine. Equity's subsidiaries contributed roughly 50% of group profit before tax — Uganda up over 500%, Tanzania up 125%, and Equity BCDC (DRC) up 58%. KCB's regional operations contributed 31% of pre-tax profit. Kenya's tier 1 banks are no longer Kenyan banks in any meaningful sense. They are East and Central African financial institutions that happen to be headquartered in Nairobi.

Asset quality also improved across the board. KCB recorded its first meaningful NPL reduction in four years. Equity's NPL ratio fell from 13.6% to 11.5%. The sector entered 2026 in better shape structurally than it has been in several years.

The Tension in the Numbers

And yet. The same high-rate environment that widened bank margins made borrowing expensive for the businesses and households those banks are supposed to serve. Kenyan SMEs have been navigating lending rates that, in many cases, remain above 20%. Credit to the private sector grew slowly. Banks, responding rationally to risk, favoured government paper and regional subsidiaries over domestic SME lending.

Record profits at the top of the banking sector and constrained productive credit at the bottom are not contradictory outcomes. In Kenya in 2025, they were the same outcome viewed from different angles.

The Bigger Picture: Capital Is Finally Moving

Step back from the quarterly results, and a more consequential story is developing across the continent.

Africa faces an estimated $402 billion annual development financing gap — the difference between what is needed to build the infrastructure and productive capacity the continent requires, and what is currently being invested. For decades, that gap was treated as a foreign aid problem. It is increasingly being treated as an investment opportunity.

The African Development Bank held its inaugural Private Capital Mobilisation Day in London in December 2025, convening over 150 senior decision-makers from private equity firms, sovereign wealth funds, pension funds, and development finance institutions. The explicit agenda: shift the conversation from dialogue to execution. AfDB president Dr Sidi Ould Tah's Four Cardinal Points vision of mobilising capital, reforming financial architecture, converting demographic growth into economic power, and building resilient infrastructure is the clearest articulation yet that the institution understands its role has to change.

Private equity inflows into Africa have grown meaningfully. Africa50, the infrastructure investment platform, surpassed $1.4 billion in managed assets in 2025. The AfDB committed $100 million to the Emerging Africa and Asia Infrastructure Fund, which is targeting over $850 million in infrastructure investment across Africa by 2027. The Zafiri vehicle — a joint AfDB and IFC initiative backed by an initial $300 million — is specifically designed to channel long-term equity into distributed renewable energy companies that mainstream capital markets have systematically underfinanced.

Why Internal Investment Is the Only Sustainable Answer

Foreign aid built roads and clinics. It did not build industries. The distinction matters enormously for what comes next.

Industrialisation in Sub-Saharan Africa requires a specific combination of inputs: reliable power, functional logistics networks, access to working capital, and a large enough domestic market to justify the fixed costs of manufacturing at scale. Infrastructure is the enabling condition for all of it. Without consistent electricity, a factory cannot run. Without roads and ports, goods cannot move at competitive cost. Without digital connectivity, financial services cannot reach the businesses and consumers who need them.

The AfCFTA — the African Continental Free Trade Area — represents a potential $3.4 trillion market. The AfDB, AfCFTA Secretariat, and Africa50 have explicitly partnered to unlock it through strategic infrastructure development. That is the right framing. Trade agreements create the legal architecture for commerce. Infrastructure creates the physical conditions in which that commerce can actually occur.

Private equity plays a different but complementary role. Where development banks provide patient, concessional capital for projects that are too risky or too long-dated for commercial investors, PE firms bring the operational expertise, governance discipline, and exit orientation that moves capital efficiently into growth-stage businesses. SMEs represent nearly 90% of businesses and over 60% of jobs on the continent, yet continue to face limited access to risk capital. The AfDB has been explicit that reinforcing African investment funds and expanding their capacity to finance small and mid-market companies is a strategic priority, not a peripheral one.

What the Banks Should Do With Their Profits

Kenya's tier 1 banks are not obligated to solve Africa's infrastructure financing gap. They are commercial institutions with shareholders, regulators, and risk frameworks to manage. But the question of where their balance sheets are pointed matters for the economies that host them.

The 99% digital transaction rate is an asset. The regional footprint across East and Central Africa is an asset. The trust that decades of deposit relationships represent is an asset. None of that is being fully deployed against the SME credit gap, the infrastructure deficit, or the financial inclusion problem that still leaves large portions of the productive economy on the outside of the formal financial system.

Record profits are a good problem to have. The more interesting question is what gets built with them.
