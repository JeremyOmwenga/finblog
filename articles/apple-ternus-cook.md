Yesterday, Apple confirmed what had been widely anticipated for months. Tim Cook will become executive chairman of the board on September 1, 2026, and John Ternus, currently senior vice president of Hardware Engineering, will become Apple's eighth CEO. The board approved the transition unanimously. Cook, now 65, will remain in the role through the summer to ensure a clean handover.

It is the first CEO transition at Apple since Cook himself took over from Steve Jobs in 2011, shortly before Jobs died. Fifteen years is a long tenure by any measure. By the measure of what he built, it is extraordinary.

## What Cook Actually Did

When Cook became CEO in August 2011, Apple's market capitalisation was approximately $350 billion. On the day the transition was announced, it closed at $4 trillion. That is a more than twenty-fold increase over fifteen years, a compounding story that requires some unpacking to understand properly.

Jobs built Apple on the premise that hardware design was the product. The device was the thing. Cook's insight, which took years to fully articulate but is now the defining logic of the company, was that the device was the entry point. The product was everything that happened after you bought it.

The services pivot is the financial story of Cook's tenure. Apple Music launched in 2015. Apple Pay the same year. Apple TV+ in 2019. Apple Arcade in 2019. iCloud had been growing steadily since 2011. By 2025, the Services segment, encompassing the App Store, Apple Pay, Apple TV+, Apple Music, and iCloud, generated $106 billion in annual revenue. That figure represents a margin profile considerably higher than hardware, and it recurs monthly, automatically, from over one billion active subscriptions across 2.3 billion active devices.

The device numbers matter because they define the addressable market for every service. More iPhones in the world means more potential Apple Music subscribers, more iCloud storage customers, more App Store revenue. The hardware and the services are not separate businesses. They are a single compounding system where each element amplifies the other.

Cook also moved Apple decisively away from dependence on third-party silicon. The M-series chip transition, which began with the M1 in 2020 across MacBook Air, MacBook Pro, and Mac Mini, gave Apple full control over the performance and power efficiency characteristics of its Mac computers. The M5 series now ships across the Mac lineup. The performance gap over Intel-based machines that existed at the time of the transition has been sustained and extended. Custom silicon is not merely a cost or performance story. It is a vertical integration story, and vertical integration is what Apple does better than almost any company in history.

## The Ecosystem as Moat

Understanding Apple's valuation requires understanding what it has built around the device, not just the device itself.

An Apple user who has been in the ecosystem for several years is not simply a customer. They are an integrated participant in a platform architecture that spans communication (iMessage, FaceTime), media (Apple Music, Apple TV+, Podcasts), productivity (Pages, Numbers, Keynote, Notes), payments (Apple Pay, Wallet), health (Health app, Apple Watch integration), file management (iCloud Drive), and security (Keychain, Face ID, Passkeys).

Every one of these surfaces works seamlessly with every other. A password saved on an iPhone appears instantly on a MacBook. A file edited on an iPad syncs to a Mac without configuration. An incoming call on an iPhone can be answered on a MacBook. AirDrop transfers files between devices in the same room in seconds. The Apple Watch unlocks the Mac when the user sits down.

This is not accidental convenience. It is a deliberately constructed switching cost. Moving from Apple to any competing platform means not just replacing a phone but unwinding years of integrated workflow, media libraries, message history, and saved credentials. Studies consistently find that Apple's customer retention rate sits above 90%. The ecosystem does not simply satisfy customers. It makes the cost of leaving prohibitive.

Privacy sits at the centre of this architecture, and Cook made it a stated competitive differentiator in a way that Jobs never did. App Tracking Transparency, introduced in iOS 14.5, required apps to ask users explicitly for permission to track them across other apps and websites. The advertising industry's reaction was immediate and hostile, which is the clearest possible signal that the feature worked as intended. Mail Privacy Protection followed. On-device processing for Face ID and increasingly for AI inference. Differential privacy in data collection. Private Relay in iCloud. The company's consistent position is that personal data should not be the product, and unlike most of its peers, its business model does not depend on advertising revenue that would create a structural incentive to say otherwise.

## Who Ternus Is

John Ternus joined Apple in 2001. He has been SVP of Hardware Engineering since 2021, overseeing the teams responsible for iPhone, iPad, Mac, Apple Watch, AirPods, and the M-series chip roadmap. He is, in the most direct sense, the person most responsible for the physical products that anchor the entire ecosystem.

He introduced the iPhone Air at a New York event in March 2026. He has been increasingly visible in international engagements, regulatory meetings, and press contexts over the past year, in what is now clearly identifiable as a deliberate preparation for the public role the CEO position requires.

Cook described him as having the mind of an engineer, the soul of an innovator, and the heart to lead with integrity. The language is characteristically Jobsian, and presumably deliberate.

The challenges Ternus inherits are real. Supply chain complexity, geopolitical exposure in a manufacturing base still heavily concentrated in Asia despite ongoing diversification, tariff pressures from a US administration that has been publicly hostile to Apple's overseas production footprint, and the AI race, in which Apple's on-device, privacy-first approach to intelligence puts it in a structurally different position from competitors willing to process user data in the cloud.

The asset he inherits is the most valuable consumer franchise ever assembled: two billion active devices, a hundred billion dollars of recurring service revenue, custom silicon that competitors cannot replicate on short timescales, and a customer retention rate that most consumer businesses would consider fictional.

Whether Ternus can extend the Cook era's compounding logic, or whether September 1 marks a genuine inflection point, is the most consequential question in consumer technology right now.

The market, at $4 trillion, is betting on continuity.
